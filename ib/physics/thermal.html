Definitions

  1. Heat refers to the mechanism of transfer of energy in a thermal interaction as a consequence of a difference in temperature of two bodies.
  2. The temperature of a body is a measure of the average random kinetic energy of the molecules.
  3. The internal energy of an object is the sum of the total random kinetic energy and the total intermolecular potential energy of its particles. The intermolecular potential energy is highly dependent on what state the object is in (solid, liquid or gas).
    • Total Internal Energy = Total Intermolecular Potential Energy + Total Random Kinetic Energy\text{Total Internal Energy = Total Intermolecular Potential Energy + Total Random Kinetic Energy} Total Internal Energy = Total Intermolecular Potential Energy + Total Random Kinetic Energy
  4. The specific heat capacity, ccc, is the energy required to change the temperature of a unit mass of a substance by one kelvin.
  5. The specific latent heat of fusion, LfL_fLf​, is the energy released or absorbed per unit mass of a substance undergoing a phase change between solid and liquid at constant temperature and pressure.
  6. The specific latent heat of vapourisation, LvL_vLv​, is the energy released or absorbed per unit mass of a substance undergoing a phase change between liquid and gas at constant temperature and pressure.
  7. A mole, nnn, of a substance is the amount of the substance that contains as many particles, such as atoms or molecules, as there are in 121212121212 grams of carbon-12.
    • The number of atoms in 121212121212 grams of carbon-12 is 6.02⋅1023 mol−16.02 \cdot 10^{23} \text{ mol}^{−1}6.02⋅1023 mol−1, the Avogadro constant, NAN_ANA​.
  8. Pressure is the magnitude of the normal – or reaction – force applied to the surface of an object per unit area over which that force is distributed.
  9. One kilocalorie is the amount of heat needed to raise the temperature of one kilogram of water by 1∘C1^\circ C1∘C.

Note: The molar mass of the substance in grams contains an amount of particles equal to the Avogadro constant.

Thermal Interactions

  • Three types of thermal interactions:
    1. Conduction: energy transfer through particle-particle collisions and movement.
    2. Convection: energy transfer through the distribution and movement of a fluid (liquid or gas).
    3. Radiation: energy transfer through electromagnetic radiation that each body emits.
  • A thermal interaction will affect the internal energy of a substance.
  • As a substance is heated up, its particles become more energetic. This is an increase in kinetic energy of the molecules.
  • Phase changes:
    • Melting: from solid to liquid, energy is absorbed by the substance.
    • Freezing: from liquid to solid, energy is released by the substance.
    • Vapourisation or boiling or evaporation: from liquid to a gas (vapour), energy is absorbed by the substance.
    • Condensation: phase change from vapour (gas), to liquid, energy is released by the substance.
  • The values of latent heat of vapourisation are significantly greater than the values for the latent heat of fusion.
  • The increase in interatomic separation of the particles from a liquid to a gas is much larger than from a solid to a liquid.

Gases

  • Gases are composed of randomly moving gas particles, which each have a mass.
    • As particles collide with walls, their change in momentum accounts for the force exerted on it.
    • The collisions of a large number of particles per unit time average out to an overall force exerted on the wall, which equates to pressure.

Ideal Gases

  • Properties
    1. Identical perfect spheres
    2. Perfectly elastic - don’t lose KE in collisions
    3. No intermolecular forces: PE does not change
    4. So small that their volume is negligible compared to the entire container.
  • As the temperature of an ideal gas increases, so does its average speed.
  • Real gases only behave like ideal gases under low pressure and high volume.
