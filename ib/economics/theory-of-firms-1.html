Theory of Firms: product, costs, revenue, and profit

Production

Definitions

  • Total product: the total output that all the units of variable input can produce.
  • Average output: the output per worker/machine.
    • Total productTotal units of variable input\frac{\text{Total product}}{\text{Total units of variable input}}Total units of variable inputTotal product​
  • Marginal product: the additional output that each additional unit of variable input can produce.
    • ΔTotal productΔUnits of variable input\frac{\Delta{\text{Total product}}}{\Delta{\text{Units of variable input}}}ΔUnits of variable inputΔTotal product​
  • Short run: a period when atleast one factor of production is fixed. All production takes place in the short run.
  • Long run: a period when all the factors of production are variable. All planning takes place in the long run.
  • Law of diminishing marginal returns: as more and more units of variable inputs are added to one or more fixed inputs, there comes a point where the additional output obtained due to the employment of one more variable input begins to decrease.

Graph properties

  • The average product(AP) curve intersects the marginal product(MP) curve at the average product(AP) curve’s highest point.
  • Diminishing marginal returns set in after the marginal product(MP) curve’s highest point, showing that the contribution of each additional worker is slowly decreasing and that the productivity is also decreasing.
  • The TP is maximum when MP = 0.

Why does MP rise and then fall with increase in variable inputs?

  • Initially, as the number of variable inputs(such as labour) increases, the labour productivity increases and therefore the rate of change of product is greater than the rate of change of variable input(number of workers), resulting in an increasing AP and MP.
  • However, there come a point after which diminishing marginal returns set in and so the additional product obtained from the employment of an additional input factor decreases. Consequently, the rate of increase of output becomes lesser than the rate of increase of variable input, leading to the MP curve falling.
  • After this, there is also a point where an additional input actually reduces the total product produced, meaning that the additional worker actually contributes negatively, leading to the MP curve becoming negative.

Costs of production

Definitions

  • Explicit cost: directly related to production and are the physical payments for the factors of production, such as wages, rent and interest.
  • Implicit cost: risks that are taken and sacrifices that are made by person or people while managing a business such as opportunity costs when making a decision.
  • Economic costs: sum of explicit costs and implicit costs.

Short run

  • In the short run, there are both fixed costs and variable costs.
  • Fixed costs: costs that do not vary as output changes.
  • Variable costs: costs that do vary as output changes.
  • Total costs: cost to produce all of a specified level of output. It is the sum of fixed costs and variable costs.
  • Average cost: cost per unit of output.
    • Total costTotal units\frac{\text{Total cost}}{\text{Total units}}Total unitsTotal cost​
  • Marginal cost: the additional cost for producing each additional unit of output.
    • ΔTotal costΔUnits\frac{\Delta{\text{Total cost}}}{\Delta{\text{Units}}}ΔUnitsΔTotal cost​

Long run

  • In the long run, there are no fixed costs as there are no fixed factors of production.

Economies of scale

  • As firms tend to grow in size, they can enjoy economies of scale, which means they can slowly reduce average costs of production as output increases.
  • Economies of scale are any decreases in long-run average costs that come about when a firm alters all of its factors of production in order to increase its scale of output.
  • Economies of scale allow a firm to enjoy increasing returns to scale.
  • Forms of economies of scale:
    • Specialization and expertise
      • Specialization of management or labour can increase productivity and increase efficiency.
    • Indivisibilities
      • Indivisibilities of capital and other efficient processes occur when machines are only available in large sizes that require large output in order to be used efficiently.
    • Marketing, advertising, or research and development economies of scale.

Diseconomies of scale

  • Diseconomies of scale are any increases in long-run average costs that come about when a firm alters all of its factors of production in order to increase its scale of output.
  • Diseconomies of scale lead to the firm experiencing decreasing returns to scale.
  • Forms of diseconomies of scale:
    • Control and communication problems.
    • Alienation and loss of identity.
    • Poor worker motivation.

Constant returns to scale

  • This occurs when output increases but costs remain constant throughout.

Why is the Average Variable Cost(AVC) curve U-shaped?

  • Initially when the number of workers increases, the rate of change of output is greater than the rate of change of workers. This means that the rate at which output is increasing is greater than the rate at which workers are increasing, leading to the average variable cost decreasing.
  • However, there comes a point where as more workers are added to a fixed quantity of capital, the output per worker, or the additional output due to the employment of another worker reduces, which is known as diminishing average returns. This leads to inefficiencies and the average variable cost increasing. This may happen if the workplace becomes too crowded and if workers begin to get into each other’s way.
  • Same can be said for the Average Total Cost(ATC) curve being U-shaped.

What is the relationship between Short-run Average Costs(SRAC) and Long-run Average Costs(LRAC)?

  • The LRAC is tangential to the SRAC curves.
  • When the LRAC is decreasing, the tangent at the SRAC is also seen to be decreasing.
  • When the LRAC is increasing, the tangent at the SRAC is also seen to increasing.
  • When the LRAC is minimum, the tangent at the SRAC is also at its minimum.

Why does Marginal Cost(MC) meet Average Cost(AC) at AC’s lowest point?

  • If marginal cost is lesser than average cost, increasing output will decrease average cost. This is because if the additional cost for producing an extra unit is lesser than the average cost of the unit, then producing the unit will lower the AC.
  • If marginal cost is greater than average cost, increasing output will increase average cost. This is because if the additional cost for producing an extra unit is greater than the average cost of the unit, then producing the unit will increase the AC.
  • Therefore, MC meets AC only at the lowest possible cost per unit because at that point AC will neither increase or decrease.

Profit

Definitions

  • Economic profit: total revenue−economic costs\text{total revenue} - \text{economic costs}total revenue−economic costs
  • Normal profit: the minimum amount of revenue that the firm must recieve so that it will keep the business running. It can also be said that normal profit is the break-even point for the firm.
    • total revenue=economic costs\text{total revenue} = \text{economic costs}total revenue=economic costs
    • Profit=0\text{Profit} = 0Profit=0
  • Positive economic profit: the firm is earns abnormal profit when price is above average total cost.
    • total revenue>economic costs\text{total revenue} > \text{economic costs}total revenue>economic costs

  • Negative economic profit: the firm is makes a loss, when price is lesser than average total cost.
    • total revenue<economic costs\text{total revenue} < \text{economic costs}total revenue<economic costs

Shutdown decisions

  • A firm will shut down in the short run if the price falls below or is equal to the average variable cost(AVC).
    • Price≤AVC\text{Price} \leq \text{AVC}Price≤AVC
  • A firm will shut down in the long run if the price falls below or is equal to the average total cost(ATC).
    • Price≤ATC\text{Price} \leq \text{ATC}Price≤ATC
