Atomic Structure

Definitions

  • Element: a substance that cannnot be broken down into simpler sunbstances by a chemical reaction.
  • Compound: a substance made by chemically combining two or more elements. It has different properties from its constituent elements.
  • Mass number(AAA): the number of protons plus the number of neutrons in an atom. Give the total number of nucleons, and often referred to as nucleon number.
  • Isotopes: atoms of the same element with differnet mass numbers.
  • Ions: when an atom loses or gains an electron, an ion is formed. Positive ions are known as cations and negative ions are known as anions.
  • Relative atomic mass(ArA_rAr​): average mass of an atom of an element, taking into account all the isotopes and their relative abundance, compared to one atom of carbon-12.
  • Wavelength(λ\lambdaλ): the distance between two successive crests or troughs.
    • Measured in metres(m).
  • Frequency(ν\nuν): the number of waves produced in one second.
    • Measured in Hertz(Hz).
  • Ground state: when an electron is in its lowest energy level.
  • Absorption spectrum: electron absorbs light photos and jumps upto an excited state of higher energy.
    • This spectrum would be black lines, representing absorbed colours on a coloured background.
  • Emission spectrum: Electron emits light photon as it jumps down to a state of lower energy.
    • This spectrum would consist of coloured lines, representing emitted colours on a black background.
  • Ionisation energy: the energy needed to remove an electron from the ground state of each atom in a mole of gaseous atoms, ions, or molecules is called ionisation energy.

Emission Spectrum

  • It is produced by excited atoms and ions as they fall back to a lower energy level.
  • Line emission spectrum is characteristic to the element. Therefore, different elements have different line spectra, and can be used to identify unknown elements.
  • Emission spectra are not continuous, but consist of seperate lines.
  • These lines converge towards the higher energy end of the spectrum.

Atomic models

Bohr’s atomic model

  • According to Bohr’s atomic model, electrons can occupy orbits of discrete energy. Energy is radiated (emitted or absorbed) in quantised amounts.

E=hμ=hcλE = h \mu = \frac{hc}{\lambda}E=hμ=λhc​

  • Where:
    • μ\muμ: frequency of the wave
    • h: planck’s constant = 6.63×10−34J6.63 \times 10^{-34} J6.63×10−34J
    • c: velocity of light = 3.00×108ms−13.00 \times 10^8 ms^{-1}3.00×108ms−1

Electronic Configuration

  • A more detailed model of the atom describes the division of the main energy level into s, p, d, and f sub-levels of successively higher energies.
    1s<2s<2p<3s<3p<4s<3d<4p<5s1s<2s<2p<3s<3p<4s<3d<4p<5s1s<2s<2p<3s<3p<4s<3d<4p<5s
  • Orbitals ranked in ascending order of their energy:
    s<p<d<fs < p<d<fs<p<d<f

sss orbitals

  • Has only 1 shape.
  • Holds maximum 2 electrons with opposite spins.
  • Spherical in shape.
  • Lowest energy orbital.

ppp orbitals

  • Dumbbell shaped
  • 3 orientations, which hold 2 electrons each, for a total of 6 electrons.
    • The orientations are: pxp_xpx​, pyp_ypy​, pzp_zpz​.
  • Dumbbell-shaped arranged at right angles with the nucleus at the center.
  • Higher in energy than ‘s’.

ddd orbitals

  • 5 orbital configurations, which hold 2 electrons each, for a total of 10 electrons.
  • Double-dumbell shaped.
  • Higher in energy than ‘p’.

fff orbitals

  • 7 possible shapes/orientations, for a total of 14 electrons.
  • Highest in energy, out of the 4.

Aufbau Principle

  • When electrons fill up the orbitals, the orbitals with the lowest energy level get filled up with electrons first.
  • The order of energy levels of orbitals is:
    • 1s<2s<2p<3s<3p<4s<3d<4p<5s1s<2s<2p<3s<3p<4s<3d<4p<5s1s<2s<2p<3s<3p<4s<3d<4p<5s

Hund’s Rule

  • In orbitals of the same energy level, electron pairing will not take place until each orbital has got 1 electron each in parallel scheme.
